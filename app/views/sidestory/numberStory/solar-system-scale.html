Story about numbers - solar system scale


Most of us have seen the following picture that is not drawn in truly real scale.

Picture source: File:Solar-system.png


Description in the summary: A model of the Solar System that shows sizes to scale as well as distances to scale, using distance and radius details from NASA's Solar System Exploration website. But it is critical to note that this image should not be considered a "scale model" as it does not depict both sizes and distances at the same scale.


Actually, we have to sacrifice either distance or size in order to make it a feasible print job, which is why many textbooks unfortunately fail to illustrate the scale of the solar system accurately.

"Most of the pictures in my books made the distance between planets seem small and easy to travel. Museums were no help either. The models they displayed usually had the sizes of the planets to scale, but the distances between them were a completly different scale, giving the impression of a rather close-knit family." - from Make a scale model of the Solar System

Here is the real scale solar system when the sun is the same size of basket ball (diameter ~ 24cm):

Picture source: Make a scale model of the Solar System

Note: the orbit diameter in inches appears to be off.

As we can see from the picture, Neptune is located around 800 meters, and Pluto is about 1km away from the sun that is basket ball size. See how small the earth is (2mm).

In an astronomical unit (AU) which represents the mean distance between the Earth and our sun, Jupiter is located at 5AU, Saturn at 10AU, Uranus at 20AU, Neptune 30AU, Plute at 40AU, and Kuiper Belt at 50AU.



We can extend further:




The closest star (Alpha Centauri) is 7,000km (New York - Rome) (63,240AU) from the sun. Compare the distance to the Pluto (1km)! The Betelgeuse is so huge, and its boundary lies in between the Mars and the Jupiter.

Voyager 1 probe (launched in 1977), which for the last several decades has been coursing through space at speeds upwards of 38,000 mph / 62,000 kph. That's over 17 kilometers per second (50 times faster than a bullet). But with that speed, Voyager 1 would still take over 70,000 years to reach Alpha Centauri!


For a given screen or a sheet of paper, it's almost impossible to draw the solar system in scale matching both the size of the planets and the distances.

We need several screens or a long tape-like paper to draw it in real scale!
